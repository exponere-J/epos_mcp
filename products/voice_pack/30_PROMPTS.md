# The 30 Voice-Dictation Prompts

*6 categories × 5 prompts. Say them in your own words; don't read them
verbatim. The structure is the scaffold, the voice is yours.*

---

## Category A — Morning intent-setting (5)

**A1. The walking intent.**
> "I'm about to start my day. The one thing I want moved by lunchtime
> is <thing>. The hardest part of moving it is <hardest part>. Give me
> three options for how to start, ranked by reversibility."

**A2. The yesterday's-lesson.**
> "Yesterday, the thing that surprised me was <thing>. It surprised me
> because <reason>. Tell me what I should do differently today based
> on that, in one sentence."

**A3. The one-email-decision.**
> "My inbox has one email I've been avoiding for three days. It's about
> <topic>. I've been avoiding it because <reason>. Help me decide: draft
> a response now, schedule a reply, or mark it not-mine."

**A4. The priority-realignment.**
> "The three things I said I'd do this week are <A>, <B>, <C>. As of
> this morning, I've actually done <done>. Ask me the questions that
> get me to commit to the next thing honestly, not optimistically."

**A5. The Monday-brief.**
> "It's Monday. Brief me on what matters this week. Don't ask me what
> I want to do — ask me what I'm avoiding, and start there."

## Category B — Content dictation (5)

**B1. The walk-and-talk essay.**
> "I want to write about <idea>. Here's how I'd explain it to a smart
> friend at dinner: <tell the story>. Turn that into a 600-word essay
> that keeps my phrasing, my examples, and my order of ideas. Don't
> make it sound professional."

**B2. The LinkedIn story.**
> "I want to post about something that happened today. Here's the
> moment: <moment>. Here's why it stuck with me: <why>. Make it a
> LinkedIn post, 3 short paragraphs, my voice, no 'excited to share.'"

**B3. The audience-specific teardown.**
> "I'm writing to <specific avatar>. I want to explain <concept> in a
> way they'd actually use. Draft 4 versions. The first is for them; the
> other three are for adjacent avatars I might also reach."

**B4. The objection-addressing post.**
> "The objection I hear most is: '<objection>.' I've never written a
> response to it publicly. Draft a response. 500 words. First-person.
> Don't defend — agree with the part of the objection that's true, and
> show the part that's missing."

**B5. The case-study talk-through.**
> "I'm telling you about <client / project>. Here's the problem: <X>.
> Here's what we did: <Y>. Here's the result: <Z>. Turn this into a
> case-study section, 400 words, with specific numbers I gave you."

## Category C — Decision journaling (5)

**C1. The pre-decision talk-through.**
> "I'm about to decide <thing>. My gut says <A>. The other option is
> <B>. Here's what I'm afraid of: <fear>. Make me defend <B> against
> myself for 60 seconds, then tell me which option matches my stated
> values better."

**C2. The post-decision record.**
> "I just decided <thing>. I chose <X>. I chose it because <reasons>.
> Record this in my decision journal, formatted so my future self can
> read it in 6 months and know whether I was right."

**C3. The regret audit.**
> "I think I made a wrong call on <past decision>. What I know now is
> <new info>. Walk me through whether I had enough info to know better
> at the time, or whether it was a genuine surprise."

**C4. The rule-crystallizer.**
> "I keep making the same mistake on <category>. Examples: <A>, <B>,
> <C>. Give me one sentence — a rule I could follow for 90 days —
> that would prevent the next one."

**C5. The anti-decision.**
> "I've been avoiding deciding <thing> for <time>. The cost of not
> deciding is <cost>. Force the decision. Give me two options with
> enough detail that I pick one inside this voice memo."

## Category D — People / relationships (5)

**D1. The pre-call briefing (voice).**
> "I have a call at <time> with <person>. Here's what I know about
> them: <context>. Here's what I want: <goal>. Give me three talking
> points and one question I should ask."

**D2. The post-call extract.**
> "I just got off a call with <person>. Here's roughly what we said:
> <dictation>. Extract: decisions made, open questions, next actions,
> and who owns what."

**D3. The relationship-temperature-check.**
> "I haven't talked to <person> in <time>. We used to be close. Draft
> three different first messages, ranging from most-casual to most-
> direct, and tell me which matches where we were when we last spoke."

**D4. The hard-conversation rehearsal.**
> "I need to tell <person> <hard message>. Rehearse it with me. You
> play <person> and push back. Keep going until I land a version I'd
> be okay sending."

**D5. The apology draft.**
> "I owe <person> an apology for <thing>. What I want to say is
> <words>. What I don't want to do is over-explain. Cut it to three
> sentences and tell me the one I'm tempted to leave in that I
> shouldn't."

---

## Category E — Learning / reflection (5)

**E1. The talk-back.**
> "I just read <thing> / heard <podcast>. Here's what stuck: <summary>.
> Ask me five questions that push past the surface of what I just
> said."

**E2. The pattern-naming.**
> "I've noticed I keep doing <behavior>. Examples: <A>, <B>. Give it a
> name, a 2-word name I can use for it, and tell me what it usually
> costs me."

**E3. The gap-finder.**
> "Here's what I think I know about <domain>: <dictation>. Point out
> the three things I just said that might not be true."

**E4. The example-mining.**
> "I'm trying to explain <concept>. My current examples are <A>, <B>.
> Give me three better examples from domains I already know (my own
> life, my clients, my history)."

**E5. The first-principles reboot.**
> "I've been assuming <assumption> about <topic>. If that assumption
> were wrong, what would I do differently? Walk me through it."

---

## Category F — Operations / ops (5)

**F1. The inbox-triage.**
> "Here's my inbox summary: <dictation>. Bucket into: reply today,
> delegate, decline, delete. Read back the bucketing."

**F2. The schedule-dispute.**
> "Today my calendar has <items>. My energy is <state>. Reshuffle the
> day so the hardest work happens in my peak window, and tell me what
> I'm going to have to move."

**F3. The standup-for-solo.**
> "I'm running a one-person standup. Yesterday I finished <A>. Today I
> want to do <B>. Blockers: <C>. Push back on whether <B> is actually
> the highest-leverage thing, based on my week's stated goals."

**F4. The admin-batch.**
> "I have ~30 minutes for admin. Here's the list: <list>. Batch them
> by mode (email, phone, writing) and tell me the order that minimizes
> context-switching cost."

**F5. The weekly retro.**
> "It's Friday. This week I <did>. This week I didn't <didn't>. What
> compound effect does this leave me with going into next week?"
