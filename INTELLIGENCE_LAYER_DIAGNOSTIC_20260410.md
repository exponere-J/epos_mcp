## PART II: THE THREE FOUNDATIONAL MODELS THAT UNDERPIN EVERYTHING

You said: *"2 models lay beneath all of the TTLG cycles. We can specialize instruct models."*

Here are the two (actually three) foundational decision-making models that every EPOS cycle depends on:

### Foundation Model 1: Constitutional Classifier
**Role:** Evaluates any action, statement, or decision against EPOS constitutional boundaries.

**Current mechanism:** Ruleset in `governance_gate.py` (20+ explicit rules).

**Why it matters:** Every mission, every content piece, every refactor, every healing action passes through this gate. If it's slow or inaccurate, the entire system bottlenecks or makes unsafe decisions.

**Specialization opportunity:** A small (7-13B) classification model trained on 500+ past decisions showing "this action violated Article X" or "this action passed gate Y." This model would:
- Catch edge cases the ruleset misses
- Learn the constitution's *intent*, not just the rules
- Run locally (fast)
- Train nightly (improve every day)
- Surface "gray zone" decisions for human review

**Recursive amplification:** As the system runs, it makes constitutional decisions. Every decision becomes a training example. The model gets smarter. The system becomes more autonomous.

---

### Foundation Model 2: Impact Predictor
**Role:** Estimates the likely downstream consequences of any decision or refactor.

**Current mechanism:** Rules-based scoring (impact = severity × blast-radius, heuristic).

**Why it matters:** Every healing action, every new capability, every priority rerank depends on understanding impact. Without it, you're flying blind—you might fix something low-impact and leave high-impact problems untouched.

**Specialization opportunity:** A small (7-13B) regression model trained on "past refactors → actual measurable impact." Takes a proposed change, outputs "this will likely improve metric A by X%, degrade metric B by Y%, affect these 3 dependent systems."

**Data source:** Every AAR contains before/after metrics. 30 days of AARs = hundreds of examples. Train the model on "what did we change?" → "what happened?" Learn causality.

**Recursive amplification:** Each mission generates an AAR. The AAR trains the next version of the Impact Predictor. The system gets better at predicting consequences.

---

### Foundation Model 3: Pattern Signal Discriminator
**Role:** Distinguishes true patterns (noise that repeats across contexts) from signal (systematic behavior worth learning).

**Current mechanism:** Count-based (3+ occurrences = promote).

**Why it matters:** The nightly upskill's entire success depends on learning the right patterns. If you promote noise as signal, the system teaches itself bad habits. If you miss true signal, the system doesn't learn enough.

**Specialization opportunity:** A small (5-9B) anomaly/classification model trained on "when we promoted a pattern, did it actually help later?" Run retroactive analysis on 60 days of pattern promotions. Score each one: "this helped", "this was neutral", "this hurt." Train the discriminator on that signal.

**Data source:** Constitution changelog + mission outcomes. "Did promoting this pattern change how often missions succeeded?"

**Recursive amplification:** Each night the upskill runs, it proposes patterns. The discriminator scores them. High-scoring patterns get promoted. The constitution evolves. Missions improve.

## PART III: THE MULTI-LAYERED RESEARCH QUESTIONS

These are not "what models should we use?" These are deeper questions that map the Intelligence Layer's actual decision architecture and surface where specialization creates compounding advantages.

### LAYER A: FOUNDATION LAYER (Governs Everything Downstream)

**Question A1: Constitutional Evolution**
- *How should the Constitutional Classifier learn when the constitution itself changes?*
- What happens when you promote a new constitutional principle (Phase 8, nightly upskill)? Does the Classifier automatically know about it, or does it keep rejecting decisions under the new principle?
- Test: Train the Classifier on weeks 1-4 of operation. In week 5, add a new constitutional principle. Does it apply the new principle, or stick with the old model?
- **Why this matters:** Constitutional governance only works if the model evolves with the constitution. If there's lag, you have decisions that follow old rules while new rules are already active.

---

**Question A2: Impact Prediction Latency**
- *When should the Impact Predictor run, and how confident must it be before routing?*
- Option 1: Run on every proposed mission. Flag high-risk changes for human review.
- Option 2: Run only on top-5-impact changes (learn to prioritize what to predict).
- Option 3: Run predictively during planning; run reactively during AAR to validate past predictions.
- **Why this matters:** Impact Prediction is expensive. You can't run it 1000 times per day. But you also can't miss high-impact changes. Where does it fit in the pipeline?

---

**Question A3: Pattern Signal Timing**
- *Should the Pattern Signal Discriminator train nightly, or less frequently?*
- Nightly: Every day it sees new patterns; every day it refines what "good pattern" means. But signal might be noisy with just 24h of data.
- Weekly: More data per training run; less noise. But you promote bad patterns for a week before detection.
- Monthly: Strongest signal; lots of data. But constitutions embedded for 30 days if bad.
- **Why this matters:** Training frequency affects quality. You need enough data to avoid overfitting but fast enough to correct mistakes.

**Question B1: Executive Briefing Semantics**
- *Should the morning briefing be generated differently based on context (day of week, calendar proximity to deadline, recent fire-fighting)?*

- **Why this matters:** Friday's briefing should feel human—responsive to context. A model trained on "Jamie's past days and her responses" could learn what matters most each day.

**Question B2: Metric Anomaly Interpretation**

- Strict: "Metric X dropped Y%. Threshold is Z%."
- Minimal context: "Metric X dropped Y%. This is unusual because it rose steadily for 14 days."
- Rich context: "Metric X dropped Y%. This correlates with event A (we published content 2 days ago) and inverse correlates with metric B (which went up). Your avatar recalibration 3 days ago might have contributed."
- **Why this matters:** Friday's job is to make you smarter. Generic anomaly alerts are noise. Contextualized anomalies are intelligence.

---

**Question B3: Thread Open-State Classification**
- *How should Friday distinguish between "deferred by choice" and "forgotten"?*
- Currently: If 3+ days deferred, it's stale.
- Option 1: Ask a classification model: "Read the thread history. Is this waiting for someone else's action (intentional deferral) or did we abandon it (forgotten)?"
- Option 2: Track explicit deferral reasons ("waiting for client response", "blocked on Stacey", "intentionally slow-burned").
- **Why this matters:** Not all deferred items are equal. Some are strategic delays. Surfacing only truly forgotten items would reduce noise in morning briefings.

### LAYER E: RECURSIVE LEARNING LAYER (How EPOS Teaches Itself)

**Question E1: Pattern-to-Constitution Translation**
- *When a pattern is promoted to the constitution, how does the system verify the translation is correct?*
- Currently: Human review (implicit).
- Option 1: Constitutional Classifier reads the pattern and the new constitutional rule. Flags if they're misaligned.
- Option 2: Iterative: Promote pattern as draft. Run for 7 days. Measure if missions following the new principle succeed more. If yes, finalize. If no, rollback.
- **Why this matters:** Bad constitutional rules embed forever. A verification step prevents permanent damage.

---

**Question E2: Knowledge Staleness Detection**
- *How should the system recognize when a pattern or procedure has stopped being true?*
- Currently: No staleness detection (patterns are promoted and stay).
- Option 1: Anomaly model: "This procedure used to work 95% of the time. Now it works 60% of the time. It's stale."
- Option 2: Drift model: "When was this procedure last updated? How much has the context changed since then?"
- **Why this matters:** Organizations learn and then get stuck following outdated playbooks. Staleness detection keeps the system adaptive.

---

**Question E3: Nightly Training Data Quality**
- *Should every AAR immediately become training data, or should AARs be reviewed before they train models?*
- Every AAR: Fast learning. But if an AAR contains a mistake, the mistake trains models.
- Filtered: Only AARs marked "high confidence" train models. Slower learning, cleaner data.
- Probabilistic: Every AAR trains with a confidence weight. Strong AARs weight heavily; uncertain AARs weight lightly.
- **Why this matters:** GIGO principle. Bad training data creates bad models. But overly conservative filtering means the system doesn't learn.

**Priority sequence:**
1. **Constitutional Classifier** (gates everything; trains nightly)

- Pattern Discriminator: Constitution changelog + outcomes

Every model should train nightly, learning from the day's operations.

## PART VI: MULTI-LAYERED RESEARCH QUESTIONS (EXECUTIVE VERSION)

If you had to pick **5 deep research questions** to answer before committing to the Intelligence Layer model specialization, they would be:

### Q1: Constitutional Drift
*"If the constitution evolves nightly through pattern promotion, but the Constitutional Classifier trains on yesterday's constitution, how do we prevent the classifier from being out-of-sync with the current governance?"*

Sub-questions:
- Should new constitutional rules activate immediately or on a 24-hour delay?
- If immediate, how does the Classifier know about the change before its retraining?
- If delayed, which decisions made during the gap should be re-evaluated?

**Why this matters:** A system where governance lags behind constitutional changes is broken. You need a clear answer before deploying these models.

---

### Q2: Model Confidence Calibration
*"When a model is trained on limited data (first 30 days of operation), how confident should it be, and when should it defer to rules-based decisions?"*

Sub-questions:
- The Constitutional Classifier sees 500 gate decisions in month 1. Is that enough to replace the ruleset?
- The Impact Predictor has 30 mission outcomes. Can it reliably predict impact for missions unlike those 30?
- How do you prevent models from confidently giving wrong answers while they're still learning?

**Why this matters:** Under-confident models don't amplify anything. Over-confident models cause failures. Calibration is critical.

---

### Q3: Data Leakage Prevention
*"As models train on operational data (AARs, decisions, logs), how do we ensure we're not training on sensitive client or business data?"*

Sub-questions:
- Sanitize all training data (remove client names, specific revenue figures)?
- Train only on structural patterns (decision types, metric relationships), not content?
- Separate client-specific models from EPOS-internal models?

**Why this matters:** You're building an adaptive system that learns from operations. If you leak data, you violate every client trust principle.

---

### Q4: Rollback & Correction Paths
*"When a model makes a bad decision (e.g., the Impact Predictor badly miscalculates impact), how quickly can we rollback and what's the recovery process?"*

Sub-questions:
- Should models have a "low-confidence flag" that triggers human review instead of action?
- If a model trains on bad data and embeds the error, how do you detect that and correct it?
- What's the maximum acceptable performance degradation before auto-rollback?

**Why this matters:** Models amplify decisions. Bad models amplify bad decisions. Rollback is your insurance policy.

---

### Q5: Competitive Advantage Durability
*"These specialized models learn from EPOS's operational data. Over time, they become finely tuned to EPOS's unique patterns. How do we prevent competitors from copying the approach, and what's our actual differentiation?"*

Sub-questions:
- The models themselves are small and open-weight. Competitors could build similar models. What can't they copy?
- Answer: The training data. EPOS's 2+ years of operational outcomes become the moat.
- What's the timeline for that moat to become defensible? 3 months? 6 months? 12 months?
- Should you be patenting the training methodology (SDFT) or the constitutional framework itself?

**Why this matters:** You're investing in a learning system. Understanding when it becomes proprietary helps you decide how hard to push.
